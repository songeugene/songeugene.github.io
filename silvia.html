Supporting mindfulness of college students


UX research + design to enhance the well-being of college students

Eugene Song • 5 min read


role :

Product Design, Research, Ideation

duration :

02.2021 – 03.2021 (5 weeks)

tools :

Figma, Adobe Photoshop, Adobe Illustrator, Typeform




Background


In 2020, COVID-19 ravaged the world and schools began to adopt a fully remote learning format after sending students home. Students had to quickly adapt to the different levels of stress that online learning and lack of social life brought. SIT ("Stay in touch") was made to support students' mental well-being during this rough time and stay connected with close friends from school.


The Double Diamond


The double diamond framework was used in this project to engage in divergent research and scope out the problem space. This was followed by convergent thinking to pinpoint the specific "How Might We" and value proposition. After nailing the key problem, divergent thinking was used again where participatory design techniques were implemented to zoom in on an ideal solution.

Source: The Double Diamond model: what is it and should you use it?



Discover: Forming Context


Brief

I was given a prompt to better understand some core challenges, goals, and needs that people have when it comes to maintaining wellness, with a particular focus on mood and mindfulness. My task was to lead a UX research and strategy process to better understand the unique needs, goals, pain points, frustrations, and areas of opportunity for people who are looking to better track their moods and/or practice mindfulness, and to identify appropriate approaches for addressing the core challenges.


Target Audience

For my mobile app's target audience, I chose college students who want to connect more with others and who need more structure in their lives because of changes due to current times. The problem is that through the global pandemic, it became hard to feel connected to friends who they can no longer see everyday and difficult to plan new activities with friends due to restrictions.

In order to address these pain points, I came up with the following goals:

• create an easy-to-use product that enhances the well-being of college students who are going through hard times
• provide a solution for students to feel more connected with friends who live far away (different countries and time zones)
• encourage spending time with friends (virtually or safely) when they need to separate from family time


User Survey and Interviews

Through user interviews and surveys, I got the following feedback about meditating/mood-tracking applications from college students.

“I really miss my friends who are all in the US right now and I hate not being able to go out as much as I want to since I’m an outgoing person. I’m trying to see this in a different perspective and take this time alone to improve myself.”


“Moodtracking is just going to be a reminder that I’m always in a bad mood.”


"I’d rather do something more productive than meditate because I feel better about myself that way."


Here are more insights gathered from the survey:
• no one who responded uses meditating or mood-tracking apps (instead they use apps that don’t necessarily have the purpose of meditating or mood-tracking like Tiktok, Nike Run, Facetime)
• most people enjoyed more outdoor, social activities before the pandemic
• most people showed that they picked up different hobbies due to the effects of the pandemic (I.e. more netflix, duolingo, reading, digital painting, cooking, knitting, etc.)

I came to realize that college students do not need or want a simple meditating app. What we need instead is a way to continue our social lives and feel productive by staying focused on our academics.


Define: Understanding the User


Consolidating my findings from user interviews, secondary research and competitor analyses, I created two personas to guide my product thinking to serve a primary demographic. Meet Sarah and Rick!



Goal: How might we?


Synthesizing the problems mentioned above as a HMW to define a goal, this became:

How might we design an app which increases productivity of college students while helping them stay connected with friends far away?


Develop: Ideate


I went through stages of creating an affinity map, 8-box brainstorm, prioritization matrix, and storyboards in order to decide what features would be best to achieve my project goals.



Deliver:


The features that I ended up prioritizing were a group calendar, live chatbox, 10-minute planner, and a daily journal.



Reflection:


As a product designer, I discovered an opportunity to apply all my knowledge and experience acquired from various coursework and projects. One key takeaway is the importance of flexibility in always remembering who I am designing for.
